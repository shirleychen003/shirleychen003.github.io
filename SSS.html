1. Overview

Have you ever had issues managing a subscription, forgotten about a free trial that you signed for, or gone through a tedious process attempting to cancel a service? Subscription services are supposed to be considered tools to facilitate everyday tasks, so why can so many problems arise within the subscriptions framework? Subscriptions Super Simplified (3S) was created with the objective to solve the aforementioned issues along with facilitating any processes while interacting with a subscription service. By using this app, users are able to oversee their subscriptions with ease and eliminate any unnecessary stresses.

This app was created as a group project for Professor Olivier St. Cyr’s class INF352: Information Design Studio II: How to Design at the University of Toronto - St. George.

2. Research

To deepen our understanding of the issues we were trying to address, my group and I conducted thorough research using both secondary and primary research methods.

Prior to conducting the primary research, we explored secondary sources to get an overview of the subject matter. Research topics consisted of services and impact on subscriptions, existing public opinions and solutions, and current business models. Our group then organized a survey through Google forms and individual in person interviews for our primary research component.

By the end of the research process, we had collected 77 anonymous responses in the Google forms, and interviewed answers from 16 different individuals. Since our target demographic was subscription users, we had two main requirements for the interviewees: they must have at least one subscription and pay for it, and they must have cancelled or tried to cancel at least one service in the past year. These interviews were conducted in person or over the phone. From our research, we were able to compile various struggles users had with their subscriptions and then pinpoint the similarities.

75% of the Google forms participants had at least forgotten to cancel their subscription once and 73% relied on their memory track their subscriptions.

Using Miro, we created an affinity map where we organized all of our interview data into sections to identify key themes. Along with the issue of forgetting to cancel their subscriptions after a free trial due to lack of tracking methods, the interviewees expressed that there is often a complicated and frustrating cancellation process involved with subscription services.

3. Ideation

An essential part in our project was the ideation process, in which my group and I were able to practice collaboration and exercise our creative thinking.

Part 1 of the ideation process included creating a persona, an empathy map, As-Is scenario, and need statements. Firstly, we generated a persona using data from our research and designed a potential scenario that would benefit from using our app. Our persona was Selena the Subscriber, a busy university student with lots of subscriptions.

To gain a deeper understanding of Selena’s thoughts, motivations, and feelings, we curated an empathy map. Quotes and expressed feelings were derived from our research data. Using the empathy map, we created an As-Is scenario to walk through a potential real world scenario that Selena would experience when facing an issue with a subscription. The mapped out As-Is scenario clearly communicated the main pain points that Selena had.

Once those were all finished, we compiled a list of need statements inspired by the major pain points. The main concerns from our primary research consisted of lack of subscription flexibility, simplicity, awareness and transparency, which we made sure to address in the need statements:

  • Selena needs a way to easily be able to cancel her subscription so that it is a simple, stress-free experience.
  • Selena needs a way to manage her subscriptions so that she is aware of the subscriptions she has.
  • Selena needs a way to modify her subscription if needed so that she is completely satisfied with what she is paying for.
  • Selena needs a way to be fully informed about the service that she is subscribing to so that transparency is practiced.

Part 2 of the ideation process consisted of an “design dash” ideation process and creating a prioritization grid and a To-Be scenario. Additionally, we applied any feedback we got from Part 1 to this part.

The “design dash” ideation process involved the group setting a 5 minute timer to write down as many solution ideas we had for the pain points that we recognized from the first part of our project. The brainstormed ideas could be both practical or absurd. When the 5 minutes was up, we drew out the generated ideas and included a small description.

The final part of the “design dash” process was to vote on each idea’s feasibility (blue dots) and impact (red dots). How did each idea expand user value? Did it alleviate pain? How feasible really is the idea? Once we finalized our votes, we used a prioritization grid to plot out the best solution to Selena’s various pain points.

Finally, to end off this thorough ideation process, we outlined a potential scenario with our persona of their actions, thoughts, and feelings using our proposed solution in a To-Be scenario and ensured that the pain points were addressed.

4. Prototypes Design and Evaluation

Following the completion of the ideation process, our team developed the first prototype, the Low-Fidelity Paper Prototype as shown below:

We made sure to include a title for each screen, annotate the sketches, and follow the outline of a Sequential Storyboard so that the flow of the app was organized and direct. We then conducted usability testing by having participants walk through the prototype and their understanding of each page, being sure to use the 10 usability heuristics. Participants were also encouraged to express their thoughts and feelings out loud, implementing the think-aloud protocol.

Our two participants had somewhat different backgrounds, which illustrated the importance of including various perspectives in our trial since different ranges of feedback were provided. However, a shared, major concern that both participants had was the management of payment with the app. They both suggested features to help with reminding to update the payment method, a way to change the payment, and a form of feedback that communicates if their subscription has already been paid for.

5. Moving Forward

The main focus of this project was to ease any stresses when interacting with subscription services. Although I had personally experienced some issues with subscriptions services in the past, I did not realize the extent of some of the issues that subscriptions users face. Doing both primary and secondary research was essential to ground my understanding around the issue, making it easier to approach the problem and have reasoning for any specific design decisions.

Every detail during the process was crucial and facilitated any future tasks that were to be done. It was important to trust the process because as thorough as each step of the process was, every step was connected and the great deal of planning was eventually beneficial in some way or another.

In the future, it would be invaluable to run usability testing with more participants to receive feedback or suggestions of any features that should be included in our application. Additionally, with more time, my group and I would have loved to consider and implement every piece of feedback that was given to us during the second round of usability testing for the Medium-Fidelity prototype.

Overall, this project was an immense milestone for me in my UX journey since it was my first, official UX case study! It taught me not only about the intricate aspects of a UX design project, but general project management skills as well. The project's journey has been educational, highlighting the value of user feedback, iterative design, and the importance of simplicity in improving user subscription management.
